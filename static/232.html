听力文本：

W: Ah, I had the most relaxing weekend!

M: Really? Tell me about it.

W: I went to visit my Aunt Ruth out in the country. You remember – you went there with me one time about four years ago in the spring. You loved the wild flowers… Remember?

M: Oh, of course! It’s beautiful there – so calm and peaceful.

W: Yes! Aunt Ruth and I rode horses all around her farm: up on the hills and down by the water – next to the small lake. Aunt Ruth rode on her big brown horse called Lucky and I rode a small white horse called Snowball.

M: That does sound like a nice weekend. I remember how quiet everything was except for the sound of birds and the wind in the trees.

W: Yes, you’re right. It’s really peaceful. And the stars at night are so bright and clear. And the best thing is that you just forget all of your worries.

M: Ummm … But don’t you worry about being out of touch? Whenever I go out of town to the country, I lose all cell phone reception and I worry nonstop that people are trying to call me. I worry that I’m missing important emails and …

W: Oh no! Absolutely not. Everyone needs a complete break from technology. You need time in nature to leave the stress of life behind. A calmness returns to you. Nature heals better than any medicine!

听力文本：

More than 100 people gather on a cold evening, talking excitedly as they stand in the dark on a hillside. There isn’t a single light around them. These people have traveled to the countryside more than an hour from the city to get away from the glow of city lights. That’s because they are attending a star gazing party.

Star gazing parties are gatherings where professional astronomers set up their telescopes and invite star-watching beginners to come and learn about the night sky. Getting away from lights from buildings in the city helps the star gazers see objects in the sky much clearer.

At the star gazing party, Dr. Brown from the city museum starts off by asking the beginners to simply look up and take in all they can see. He points out for them plenty of things that can be seen without special equipment. “Stars and satellites can be found if you know where to look,” Dr. Brown tells them.

After Dr. Brown’s guided tour, several dozen professional astronomers offer closer views. Each has focused their telescope on a different part of the sky. As the beginners take a look through the telescope, the astronomers give them a mini-lesson about the stars.

Dr. Brown tells the beginners that they can have their own star gazing party at home and learn a lot just by paying attention to what’s happening up above. “Watch the sky as the seasons pass, and you will see that it changes,” he says. “Or start with the moon. Notice when and where you are seeing it – maybe even in the early morning while you wait for the bus.”

听力文本：

Joanna, a southern Arizona farmer, said on Thursday that she watched in horror as thousands of bees surrounded a 1,000-pound pig during an attack that lasted about two hours.

“I went out three times to pour water over the pig,” Joanna told the journalist. “The very last time I went, he lifted his head when I called his name, moved his ears a bit, and that was the end.”

This happened when farmers were trying to move a bees’ nest that weighed around 200 pounds. About 250,000 killer bees gathered together like a black cloud and attacked both animals and workers.

Joanna said the attack killed her male pig, Franklin. Nearby Franklin, an 800-pound pregnant pig was also attacked so many times that she became unconscious and lost her babies.

Bee expert Dr. Smith explained why Franklin had no chance. “When killer bees attack an animal, they will release a special chemical which signals other bees to join the attack. In this case, when a few killer bees started to attack Franklin, they spread the special chemical on the pig and that made Franklin the target for the rest of the bees to attack,” Dr. Smith said. “So the rest of the bees went completely crazy and killed Franklin, the poor pig. We watched him take his last breath.”

Dr. Smith said he can’t explain why the bees are so disturbed this year. “It doesn’t make any sense,” Dr. Smith said. “I’ve been doing this over 20 years. It’s true that killer bees are more likely to attack when they perceive a danger. But for some reason, this year, the killer bees are just going completely crazy.”
